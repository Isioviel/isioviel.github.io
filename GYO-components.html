Big Components

There are various large or long things in your kit!

  • Breadboard - used to build your Arduino circuits.
  • Power supply module - used to power your Arduino without using your computer (not in the picture).
  • Jumper cables - these cables have pointy ends, known as male or M, which can fit in the breadboard and non-pointy ends, known as female or F, which can connect to your Arduino.
  • Pin headers - these can push into the breadboard so that you can use cables with an F end, or are often soldered to components.

Two-legged Components

Most things in your kit have two legs, so you will need to use some detective work to figure out which is which! Look at the shape, colour, and any numbers or identifying marks.

Light-emitting diodes, LEDs, come in lots of colours. They only work when connected the right way round.

Resistors limit the current flowing through a circuit. You will have lots of different values - work out which is which by following a colour chart!

Thermistors are temperature-dependent resistors. You can use them to add temperature control into your program.

Photodiodes are light-dependent resistors. You can use them to add light control into your program.

Buzzzers come in two varieties, active and passive. Active buzzers will make a sound as soon as you connect power to them, passive buzzers need a signal (they won't do anything unless you tell them to in your code).

Active buzzers have a positive and negative leg, passive buzzers will work in either direction.

Diodes limit current flow to one direction only. LEDs are a kind of diode (which is why they only work in one direction!).

Capacitors store electrical energy for later use. They can help keep your circuits running smoothly if you are drawing a lot of power!

There are two kinds of capacitor, ceramic and electrolytic. Electrolytic capacitors only work in one direction (they have a positive and negative leg), ceramic capacitors work either way round.

Three-legged Components

Potentiometers are also called variable resistors - they can be used as a 'dial' to control your program.

Transistors come in various kinds! They can be used as electrical switches or as amplifiers, depending on which ones you have.

In your kit, you have a PN2222 transistor. An example of its use, to control a motor, can be found in this Adafruit lesson.

Many-legged Components (Chips)

To identify an electronic chip, you will need to look for a identifying code, usually printed on the top.

You might have these chips in your kit...

  • 74HC595, 16 legs - this is a 'shift register'. An example of its use, to control eight LEDs from one Arduino pin, can be found in this Adafruit lesson.
  • 4N35, 6 legs - this is an 'optocoupler' and can be used to reduce electrical interference.
